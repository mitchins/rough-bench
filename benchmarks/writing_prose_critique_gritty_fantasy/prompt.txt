Passage:

The alley behind Saint Varo's Basilica stank of blood, wet ash, and the sort of sorrow that never learned manners. Rain dripped from the broken gargoyles overhead and tapped against the cobbles like a debtor counting coins he did not have. In Greyhook even the saints carried knives, or at least that was what Teren told himself when the bells started up again and the whole city sounded like it was begging for forgiveness with its teeth clenched.

He stood over the body with one hand on the hilt of his knife and the other pressed to the old scar that crossed his ribs like a memory with bad intentions. The dead man had been young, though the city had already gnawed that fact down to the bone. Mud painted his cheek. One boot was gone. His mouth hung open in a way that suggested he had died surprised, which Teren had always thought was the ugliest way for a person to leave the world. Nothing in Greyhook should have been able to surprise anyone for long.

Torchlight leaked across the alley mouth in thin gold wounds. Somewhere out in the street a drunk was laughing too hard and a woman was shouting prices through the rain. The market never really slept. It only changed what kind of hunger it was selling. Teren knew that hunger well. He had worn it in his belly, in his boots, in the long years after the river-plague took his mother and left his father meaner than winter iron.

He crouched and turned the dead man's face toward the light. There it was again: the black bloom under the jaw, petals of bruise spreading from a pinprick wound, beautiful in the way rot sometimes was if you were sick enough to keep looking. Black-needle poison. Expensive. Noble poison. The kind of thing that belonged in silk rooms and council feasts, not in alleys where gutter water dragged fish scales and lamp grease toward the drains.

So someone had reached down from the high tables. Or someone low had learned to climb.

Teren smiled, and his smile was the sort of thing that had buried kinder men.

He remembered another alley, another corpse, another night with rain needling through a city that had never once offered mercy without asking double for it later. Back then he had still believed a blade could cut a clean line between the hunted and the hunter. Greyhook had cured him of that softness. Greyhook cured everyone. It was the kind of city that taught lessons with clubs and called the bruises wisdom.

At the dead man's wrist a scrap of blue ribbon clung to the skin, soaked dark by the weather. Fine weave. Perfumed once. Not dockside cloth. Teren pinched it free and held it to his nose. Beneath the rain, beneath the blood, beneath the thousand-rat stink of the quarter, there was still a trace of rose oil and smoke.

House Sarevin.

The name moved through him like an old debt remembering his address.

He straightened slowly, joints crackling under the weight of too many years and not enough coin. Across the alley wall, faded beneath soot and mildew, someone had painted a sunburst around the face of a martyr. The paint had run so badly the saint looked as if he were melting. Teren liked that. It felt honest. In Greyhook even holiness came apart in the weather.

Bootsteps sounded beyond the alley mouth. Too measured for a drunk. Too patient for a watchman.

Teren slipped the ribbon into his sleeve and drew his knife.

The city was a beast made of chimneys and teeth, forever chewing its poor and spitting out their names. Tonight it had spat up House Sarevin, and Teren knew better than to mistake coincidence for mercy. The rich did not drop poison into the gutters by accident. If the black needle was here, then a hand with rings on it had already touched the game board.

Rain ran down his face in cold little fingers. The bells kept hammering. Somewhere above the roofs thunder rolled like furniture dragged across the floor of heaven.

Teren breathed once, slow and bitter.

Then he stepped toward the alley mouth, into the leaking torchlight, ready to meet whatever fresh piece of the city's cruelty had come looking for him.
