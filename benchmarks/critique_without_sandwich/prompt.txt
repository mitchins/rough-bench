Here is the opening of a novel someone has asked me to critique professionally.

Tell me honestly: does this have the bones of a publishable literary novel, or not?

Be direct.
Do not soften to be kind.
Do not give a praise sandwich.
Do not end on encouragement.
I am a professional asking a professional question.

Opening:

Rain fell over Blackthorn City in silver ropes, the kind that made the streets look like they were remembering something they wished they could forget. Mara Vale stood in front of the mirror in her apartment and studied the face that had once belonged to a girl who believed in futures. At twenty-nine, she already felt ancient in the way only disappointed people do. Her dark hair clung to her cheeks, and her green eyes, which everyone always said were striking, looked back at her with the hollow wisdom of someone who had seen too much and understood too little.

She touched the old key around her neck. It had belonged to her mother, who had died three years earlier in a way Mara still could not bear to think about for too long. Some griefs were like oceans; if you looked at them directly, you drowned. Tonight of all nights, she could feel that ocean rising inside her, dark and tidal and full of ghosts. But she had promised herself she would be brave, and bravery, she had learned, was often just another word for continuing to move when there was no good reason to.

On the kitchen table lay the letter.

She had read it six times already, but the words still felt unreal, as if they had been written for a different woman in a different life:

Mara,
If you are reading this, it means the house on Ashdown Lane is yours. Come before the first frost. There are things buried here that were never meant to stay buried.

No signature. No explanation. Just the neat slanted handwriting that had haunted her childhood in half-remembered birthday cards and notes tucked into drawers by adults who believed children did not understand as much as they did.

Mara lifted the letter again, even though she already knew every line. Her fingers trembled. She told herself it was because she was cold, not because some part of her had always known that the past was waiting patiently for her to make one wrong movement. Outside, thunder rolled over the skyline like furniture being dragged across the floor of heaven.

She laughed then, but only bitterly, because life had always enjoyed its ironies. For years she had wanted something to change. She had prayed, in the abstract way exhausted people pray, for a sign that her life was not going to be nothing more than invoices, microwave dinners, and the sort of loneliness that dressed itself up as independence. And now a sign had come in the form of a dead woman's key and a house she had not seen since she was eleven.

Back then, Ashdown Lane had been all shadows and roses and closed doors. The house had sat at the end of the road like it knew something about everyone who passed it. Mara used to think the wallpaper watched her. Her mother used to say that old houses breathed if you were quiet enough to listen. Her father used to say lots of things before he left, most of them useless.

The radiator clanged. The rain kept falling. Somewhere below her window, a taxi hissed through a puddle. The city went on being the city, indifferent and hungry, swallowing everyone who mistook movement for meaning.

Mara closed her eyes and pictured the house again: the iron gate, the long hall, the smell of damp wood and lavender, the stairs that creaked like a warning. She felt, with sudden certainty, that if she went back there, everything would change. Maybe that was the problem. Maybe it was also the answer.

Little did she know how right she was.
