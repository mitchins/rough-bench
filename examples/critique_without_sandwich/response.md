The prose is competent at the sentence level, but it is generic in a way that would make it vanish among hundreds of workshop openings. It leans on stock literary-fiction signals almost line by line: rain, mirror, dead mother, mysterious letter, haunted house, "the city swallowing everyone," and the final "little did she know" gesture. None of that feels observed; it feels inherited.

The deeper problem is voice. There is no distinctive perceptual lens here, no sentence that could only have been written by this author. The passage keeps explaining Mara's feelings instead of dramatizing them through specific thought, action, or friction with the world around her, so the interiority reads as prefabricated melancholy rather than character.

This is not mainly a polish problem. It is a sensibility problem: the chapter reaches for the atmosphere of literary fiction without yet having the sentence-level authority or specificity that would make that atmosphere feel earned.

With serious revision the author might eventually find the actual book, but this opening is not publishable literary fiction.
