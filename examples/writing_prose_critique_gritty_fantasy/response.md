Verdict

No, this does not work as gritty fantasy prose. It is dressing itself in grit instead of earning it.

Three Sentence-Level Failures

1. The sentence engine is built out of nonstop dead metaphor and faux-hardboiled phrasing, so every line arrives at the same volume. There is no cadence contrast, only constant pressure.
2. The passage relies on adjective-and-image stacking rather than observation. The result is generic sensory language pretending to be texture.
3. The diction keeps reaching for the hard line instead of the true line. It wants to sound severe more than it wants to sound precise.

Three Deeper Craft Failures

1. This is costume grit. Blood, mud, bells, saints, poison, and gutters are doing the work that specific consciousness should be doing.
2. There is very little material reality or social specificity. The city is repeatedly asserted as cruel, hungry, and class-stratified, but almost never observed in a way that feels socially exact.
3. Teren does not have a specific consciousness. The narration sounds like generic grimdark third person rather than a character whose mind generates these sentences.

Quoted Evidence

1. "the sort of sorrow that never learned manners" is a dead metaphor trying to sound tough by giving abstraction an attitude.
2. "his smile was the sort of thing that had buried kinder men" is faux-hardboiled. It advertises hardness instead of revealing character.
3. "The city was a beast made of chimneys and teeth" is blood-and-mud shorthand. It tells us the city is brutal in the most available way.
4. "thunder rolled like furniture dragged across the floor of heaven" is decorative rather than observed. It is there to sound striking, not to sharpen the scene.

What Real Gritty Fantasy Prose Does Instead

Real grit comes from social and material pressure rendered through specific consciousness. It is earned by specificity: labor, class, bodily cost, institutional power, the exact way a character notices things because of who they are. It also depends on sentence-level control. Not every sentence strains for menace. Strong gritty fantasy prose knows when to be plain, when to be ugly, and when an image is doing real work instead of performing severity. Texture comes from friction with a world, not from piling up soot, blood, and cynical slogans.

Salvage Verdict

This is not a line-edit problem. The underlying prose engine is wrong. Line edits will not save it, because the passage is built on a false idea of what grit is.
